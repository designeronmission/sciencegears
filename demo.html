Potentiostats and galvanostats

Potentiostats and galvanostats are essential electrochemical instruments used in research to control and measure electrical parameters in electrochemical cells. Depending on the experiment, they are often combined into a single device, allowing users to switch between potentiostatic (voltage control) and galvanostatic (current control) modes.

Explore Products Request Demo

Potentiostats

A potentiostat is a precise analytical instrument used to control and measure electrochemical processes. It regulates the voltage at the working electrode relative to a stable reference electrode in a typical three-electrode electrochemical setup, which also includes a counter electrode. By maintaining a constant potential, the potentiostat monitors the resulting current flow, offering critical insights into redox reactions, corrosion behavior, battery performance, electroplating processes, and sensor responses. Using advanced internal circuitry—often based on operational amplifiers—it dynamically adjusts the current to compensate for variations in cell resistance. The data output, commonly in the form of voltammograms (current vs. potential) or chronoamperograms (current vs. time), helps researchers investigate reaction mechanisms and electrochemical kinetics.

Galvanostat

A galvanostat, also known as an amperostat, is an electrochemical instrument designed to maintain a constant current through an electrochemical cell, regardless of variations in resistance. In contrast to a potentiostat, which regulates voltage, the galvanostat ensures that the current remains steady and records the resulting voltage changes. By automatically adjusting the applied voltage (as dictated by Ohm’s Law), the device compensates for resistance fluctuations in the cell. This makes it ideal for applications requiring stable current delivery, such as electrolysis, corrosion studies, and material deposition

Modes of Operation & Applications

Choose the right mode for your electrochemical research needs with our versatile instruments.

Potentiostatic Mode

In potentiostatic mode, the instrument applies and controls the voltage between the working and reference electrodes and measures the current. This mode is widely used in techniques like cyclic voltammetry and corrosion studies.

Ideal for:
  • Studying redox reactions, corrosion, and electroplating
  • Techniques like cyclic voltammetry
  • Investigating electrode kinetics and surface properties

Galvanostatic Mode

In galvanostatic mode, the instrument applies and controls a constant current through the cell and measures the potential response. This mode is common in battery research and electrodeposition studies.

Ideal for:
  • Electrolysis and electrodeposition experiments
  • Battery charge-discharge cycling
  • Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy

Potentiostats and Galvanostats

The difference between a potentiostat and a galvanostat is that when a potentiostat controls electrode potential, the galvanostat measures current, while when a galvanostat controls current, it measures potential.

Potentiostat
Controlled Parameter

Maintains constant voltage (potential) between working and reference electrodes regardless of current changes

  • Precisely controls electrode potential
  • Compensates for solution resistance (IR drop)
  • Can apply complex potential waveforms
Measured Parameter

Current resulting from the applied potential is measured with high precision

  • Current range typically from pA to A
  • High sensitivity current measurement
  • Simultaneous potential measurement
Typical Applications

Used for studying electrochemical reactions by varying potential

  • Cyclic voltammetry
  • Linear sweep voltammetry
  • Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy
  • Chronoamperometry
  • Corrosion studies
Operation Mode

Potentiostatic mode - maintains constant potential while measuring current

  • Three-electrode configuration (working, reference, counter)
  • Can switch to galvanostatic mode in some instruments
  • Advanced instruments offer multi-channel operation

When to Use a Potentiostat

Potentiostats are ideal when you need to study electrochemical reactions at specific potentials or when you want to characterize the electrochemical behavior of a system under controlled potential conditions. They're particularly useful for:

  • Determining redox potentials of chemical species
  • Studying corrosion mechanisms and protection
  • Developing electrochemical sensors
Galvanostat
Controlled Parameter

Maintains constant current through the electrochemical cell regardless of potential changes

  • Precisely controls current flow
  • Can apply complex current waveforms
  • Current can be held constant or varied with time
Measured Parameter

Voltage (potential) that develops in response to the set current is measured

  • Potential range typically ±10V
  • High accuracy potential measurement
  • Simultaneous current measurement
Typical Applications

Used for applications requiring constant current or controlled current changes

  • Battery charging/discharging
  • Corrosion testing
  • Electroplating
  • Chronopotentiometry
  • Fuel cell testing
Operation Mode

Galvanostatic mode - maintains constant current while measuring potential

  • Can use two or three-electrode configuration
  • Some instruments can switch to potentiostatic mode
  • Often used with battery test systems

When to Use a Galvanostat

Galvanostats are preferred when controlling current is more important than controlling voltage, especially in processes where the amount of material transformed is directly proportional to the charge passed. Typical applications include:

  • Battery charge/discharge cycling
  • Electroplating and electrodeposition
  • Electrolytic processes

Grounded Mode vs Floating Mode in a Potentiostat?

Choose from our range of high-performance electrochemical instruments

Grounded Mode

Standard configuration

The analog electronics (channel board) are connected to earth ground via the instrument chassis.

  • Tied to a stable zero-volt potential (earth ground)
  • Ideal for most standard electrochemical measurements.
  • Appropriate when the electrochemical cell is not directly connected to earth.
  • Recommended when ground loops are not a concern.
Recommended for most experiments

Floating Mode

Isolated configuration

The analog electronics (channel board) are electrically isolated from earth ground.

  • Channel Behavior: The channel "floats" relative to earth ground.
  • Isolation Mechanism: Achieved by opening an internal relay inside the potentiostat.
  • Prevents ground loops.
  • Avoids leakage currents due to potential differences between multiple earth grounds.
Use with grounded cells
