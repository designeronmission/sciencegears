Potentiostats and Galvanostats – FAQs

Electrochemistry Fundamentals

Electrochemistry is the branch of physical chemistry that studies the relationship between electrical potential and chemical change, with particular focus on:

  • Redox reactions (electron transfer processes)
  • Interconversion of chemical and electrical energy
  • Behavior of ions in solution (electrolytes)
  • Properties of electrodes and electrochemical cells

Key applications include batteries, fuel cells, corrosion studies, electroplating, and electrochemical sensors.

Nernst Equation: Fundamental to electrochemistry
E = E° - (RT/nF)lnQ
where E is cell potential, E° is standard potential, R is gas constant, T is temperature, n is number of electrons, F is Faraday's constant, and Q is reaction quotient.

Potentiostats

A potentiostat is an electronic instrument that controls the voltage difference between a working electrode and a reference electrode in an electrochemical cell. It measures the resulting current flow through the counter electrode.

Key components include:

  • Control amplifier: Maintains desired potential
  • Electrometer: Measures current with high sensitivity
  • Signal generator: Produces voltage waveforms for experiments
  • Three-electrode system: Working, reference, and counter electrodes

A potentiostat operates using a feedback control system to maintain precise potential control while measuring current:

  1. Potential application: The user sets the desired potential between working electrode (WE) and reference electrode (RE)
  2. Feedback loop: The control amplifier compares the measured potential to the set potential
  3. Current adjustment: The system adjusts current through the counter electrode (CE) to maintain the desired potential
  4. Current measurement: The resulting current is measured with high precision
Key Technical Specifications:
  • Potential range: Typically ±10V
  • Current range: pA to A (depending on model)
  • Bandwidth: Important for fast measurements
  • Compliance voltage: Maximum voltage the potentiostat can apply

Modern potentiostats often include additional features like impedance measurement (EIS), multiple channels, and advanced software control.

Potentiostats enable various electrochemical measurement techniques:

Technique Description Applications
Cyclic Voltammetry (CV) Potential is swept linearly and then reversed Redox potential determination, reaction mechanisms
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) Small AC signal applied over range of frequencies Interface characterization, corrosion studies
Chronoamperometry Fixed potential with current measured over time Catalyst studies, sensor development
Linear Sweep Voltammetry (LSV) Potential swept linearly in one direction Corrosion studies, electrochemical synthesis
The choice of technique depends on the specific electrochemical information needed for your research.

Potentiostats vs Galvanostats

The difference between a potentiostat and a galvanostat is that when a potentiostat controls electrode potential, the galvanostat measures current, while when a galvanostat controls current, it measures potential.

Feature Potentiostat Galvanostat
Controlled Parameter Controls and maintains a constant voltage (potential) between the working and reference electrodes Controls and maintains a constant current flowing through the electrochemical cell
Measured Parameter Measures the current resulting from the applied potential Measures the voltage (potential) that develops in response to the set current
Typical Use Used to study electrochemical reactions by varying potential and observing current response (e.g., cyclic voltammetry) Used when a constant current is required, such as in battery charging or corrosion testing
Operation Mode Name Potentiostatic mode Galvanostatic mode

Potentiostatic mode: The instrument applies and controls the voltage between the working and reference electrodes and measures the current. This mode is widely used in techniques like cyclic voltammetry and corrosion studies.

Galvanostatic mode: The instrument applies and controls a constant current through the cell and measures the potential response. This mode is common in battery research and electrodeposition studies.

In some applications, such as Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS), potentiostatic mode is preferred for high-impedance systems (e.g., coatings, corrosion-resistant materials) to avoid large currents, while galvanostatic mode is better suited for low-impedance devices like batteries to prevent damage and maintain stable current.

Grounded Mode

In grounded mode, the analog electronics of the potentiostat (the channel board) are connected to the earth ground through the instrument chassis. This means the system reference is tied to a stable zero-volt potential (earth ground). This mode is suitable for most standard electrochemical measurements where the cell is not directly connected to earth or when ground loops are not a concern.

Floating Mode

In floating mode, the analog electronics (channel board) are electrically isolated from the earth ground, meaning the channel "floats" concerning the earth. This isolation is achieved by opening an internal relay inside the potentiostat, which disconnects the channel ground from earth ground, preventing ground loops and leakage currents caused by potential differences between multiple earth grounds.

When to use floating mode?
  • When the electrochemical cell or sample is directly connected to earth ground, such as measurements inside an autoclave, pipelines, or grounded metal structures.
  • When auxiliary instruments (e.g., oscilloscopes) connected to the cell outputs are earth grounded, to avoid ground loops.
  • When the environment or setup involves multiple earth grounds with different potentials, which can cause measurement errors due to leakage currents.
Selectable Floating

Some advanced potentiostats (e.g., VIONIC by Sciencegears) offer selectable floating, allowing the user to choose whether the working electrode, counter electrode, or cell body is grounded or floating. This flexibility enables configuring the grounding scheme to best suit the experimental setup without hardware modifications, enhancing measurement reliability and versatility.

Aspect Grounded Mode Floating Mode
Channel board connection Connected to Earth's ground Electrically isolated from Earth
Use case Standard cells, no earth connection Cells connected to earth, autoclaves, and pipelines
Advantage Simpler setup, stable reference Avoids ground loops, leakage currents
Disadvantage Possible ground loops in complex setups Slightly more complex, requires isolation
